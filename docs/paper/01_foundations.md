## Abstract

Across the natural and human sciences, stable structure repeatedly arises from the interaction of dynamic processes constrained by environmental, geometric, or informational boundaries. From atomic lattices and chemical bonding to neural synchronization and institutional governance, the emergence of persistent order appears to follow a common structural principle.

This paper introduces Coherence Under Constraint (CUC), a cross-disciplinary framework proposing that stable structures emerge when dynamic systems achieve phase-aligned coherence under boundary conditions that restrict possible configurations.

CUC does not seek to replace existing theories in physics, biology, or systems science. Instead, it provides a meta-theoretical architecture capable of integrating disparate domains of knowledge under a shared structural logic.

The framework establishes:

- formal definitions of coherence and constraint  
- a universal architecture of constraint layers  
- three governing laws of coherence formation  
- a cross-scale mapping of emergence from physics to civilization  

The paper proposes that structure itself is the product of coherence stabilized by constraint, and that this principle may represent a fundamental organizing pattern of complex systems across scales.

## 1. Introduction

The history of science reveals a recurring tension between specialization and integration.

Over the past four centuries, scientific progress has been driven largely by disciplinary fragmentation. Physics, chemistry, biology, economics, and sociology have each developed their own explanatory frameworks, mathematical tools, and methodological traditions.

This specialization has yielded immense explanatory power within domains. Yet it has also produced a persistent challenge:

**Many structural patterns appear repeatedly across disciplines but lack a unified explanation.**

### Examples

| Domain                     | Structural Phenomenon     |
|--------------------------|--------------------------|
| Condensed Matter Physics | Crystal lattices         |
| Chemistry                | Molecular bonding        |
| Biology                  | Cellular organization    |
| Neuroscience             | Neural synchrony         |
| Ecology                  | Trophic networks         |
| Sociology                | Institutional stability  |
| Economics                | Market equilibria        |

Each system contains distinct components and operates within different substrates. However, the process by which stable structure arises exhibits striking similarity.

Dynamic elements interact, oscillate, exchange energy or information, and eventually settle into persistent configurations.

These configurations remain stable only when boundary conditions constrain the possible behaviors of the system.

The Coherence Under Constraint framework proposes that these recurring phenomena reflect a single structural principle governing the emergence of order.

## 2. Historical Background

The intellectual foundations of the CUC framework emerge from several research traditions.

### Thermodynamics

The second law of thermodynamics describes the tendency toward entropy, yet local order frequently emerges in open systems.

Ilya Prigogine’s work on dissipative structures demonstrated that systems far from equilibrium can spontaneously produce ordered states.

Examples include:

- convection cells  
- chemical oscillations  
- biological metabolism  

These systems maintain order through continuous energy flow.

### Complexity Science

Research in complexity theory has identified several mechanisms for emergent structure:

- attractor states  
- self-organization  
- feedback loops  
- nonlinear dynamics  

Complex systems often display spontaneous order without centralized control.

### Systems Theory

Systems theorists have emphasized the importance of:

- boundary conditions  
- feedback mechanisms  
- hierarchical organization  

Systems remain stable when constraints regulate internal dynamics.

### Network Science

Recent work in network theory shows that topology itself acts as a constraint on system behavior.

Examples include:

- neural networks  
- social networks  
- internet infrastructure  

Network structure determines possible pathways for information flow.

### Gap in Existing Research

While these traditions describe different mechanisms of order formation, none provides a single conceptual framework capable of linking them across all scales of organization.

The CUC framework attempts to fill this gap.

## 3. Core Concepts

### 3.1 Substrate
A substrate is the medium within which interactions occur.

### 3.2 Dynamic Elements
Components that generate system behavior (atoms, cells, individuals).

### 3.3 Oscillation
Repeated variation in system state over time.

### 3.4 Phase
Relative timing relationship between oscillatory elements.

### 3.5 Coherence
Stability of phase relationships across interacting components.

### 3.6 Constraint
Boundaries limiting system states across physical, biological, and social domains.
